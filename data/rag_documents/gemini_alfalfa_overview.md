Alfalfa (
Medicago sativa L.) is a high-water-use forage crop requiring 20 to 46 inches of water per growing season, with water demand peaking during active growth and warmer periods (1.5–2.0 inches/week in summer). Precision irrigation management uses CWSI and NDVI to balance water stress, yield, and quality, especially under drought. 
Alfalfa Water Guidelines

    Total Requirement: 20–46 inches per season, depending on climate, yield, and cuttings.
    Seasonal Needs: High water usage, often requiring 1.5 to 2.0 inches per week during peak summer.
    Watering Strategy:
        Prior to Cutting: A light irrigation immediately before cutting boosts tonnage by enabling faster regrowth.
        Post-Cutting: Water usage dips slightly after cutting but rises rapidly, requiring consistent supply.
        Drought Management: Alfalfa is drought-tolerant, but 35–50% soil moisture depletion is recommended during peak periods to maintain high yield, while 50–65% is acceptable in early/late season.
    Water Saving: Alternative partial root-zone drip irrigation (PRI) can significantly reduce water usage (saving up to 67% in some trials) while maintaining acceptable quality.

    Optimal CWSI Range: A well-watered crop typically has a CWSI value near or below 0.2.
    Water Stress Thresholds: CWSI values for alfalfa often show a single-peak curve, peaking near 13:00 (midday).
        Low Stress: CWSI < 0.2
        Moderate Stress: 0.2 < CWSI < 0.4
        High Stress/Irrigation Needed: CWSI > 0.4.
    Monitoring: Infrared thermometers (IRTs) or UAS/drone thermal cameras should be used to measure canopy temperature, particularly between 13:00–16:00, to calculate CWSI.
    Performance: CWSI provides a more responsive indicator of water stress in alfalfa than soil moisture sensors alone. 

Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) for Alfalfa 
NDVI is used to monitor canopy vigor and biomass growth, particularly to assess regrowth after cutting. 

    Use in Management: NDVI is effective for calculating the Normalized Difference Red Edge (NDRE) and Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) to monitor crop growth stage and predict biomass production.
    Limitations: NDVI is less sensitive to rapid, transient changes in water stress compared to CWSI.
    Application: It is best used alongside CWSI to monitor biomass production (NDVI) alongside water stress (CWSI) to optimize irrigation. 

Integrated Management Guidelines

    Delineate Zones: Use UAV-based NDVI to define zones of high/low biomass.
    Monitor Stress: Use thermal images (CWSI) to check for canopy stress within those zones.
    Schedule Irrigation: Increase irrigation in areas where CWSI > 0.4.
    Manage Harvest: Use NDVI and plant height to predict biomass and optimize cutting times.
